## The Story

On December 6, 1912, in the ancient Egyptian city of Amarna, German archaeologist Ludwig Borchardt unearthed a painted limestone bust from the buried workshop of the royal sculptor Thutmose. It had not seen light for over **3,300 years**.

The face belonged to **Nefertiti** — "the beautiful one has come" — Great Royal Wife of Pharaoh Akhenaten. Together they led Egypt's most radical religious revolution, overthrowing the old gods and building an entire new capital from the desert floor. For a brief, blazing moment, she was the most powerful woman on Earth.

This bust was never meant for public display — it was a **sculptor's master model**, a reference kept in Thutmose's workshop for apprentices to copy. When Akhenaten's city was abandoned, the workshop was sealed, burying the bust under centuries of protective sand. That accident of history is why it survives in near-perfect condition, with one haunting detail: the **left eye was never finished**.

Today she resides in the **Neues Museum, Berlin**, having survived two world wars, Nazi looting, and Cold War division. She has become the Mona Lisa of the ancient world.

## Technical Highlights
- **Engine:** Three.js with PBR rendering, shadow mapping, and ACES filmic tone mapping.
- **Model:** Real photogrammetric scan of the Nefertiti Bust, CC BY 4.0.
- **Interactivity:** Orbit controls with smooth damping, multi-light setup (key/fill/rim).
- **Responsiveness:** Fully optimized for mobile and desktop viewing.

*Model: Nefertiti Bust, derived from photogrammetric scans. Original: Egyptian Museum of Berlin, Inv. No. 21300.*
